# Heather Honey (Formatted Overview)

Heather Honey comes from two main heather groups: Calluna vulgaris (Ling Heather) and Erica species
(Bell and winter heathers). The differences in species, bloom timing and habitat result in significant
variations in flavor and aroma. Heather Honey is intensely aromatic, highly flavored and often considered
an acquired taste, cherished by those who appreciate its complexity.

Heather landscapes thrive in wild, uncultivated, acidic soils across northern and western Europe. Dense
stands generate powerful nectar flows and are central to traditional beekeeping practices in Scotland,
Ireland, England, Norway and parts of France and Germany. The honey's thixotropic, jelly-like structure
distinguishes true Ling Heather Honey and requires special extraction methods.

The Calluna Heather is from the genus Calluna, while the Bell heathers belong mostly to the genus Erica. Both Calluna and Erica are in the Ericaceae family, one of two important families of heath or heather Community plants. These plants grow naturally in wild uncultivated lands with acidic and mineral poor soils. Mountains and mountainsides, moors, bogs, meadows, cliff-tops and even as a pioneer plant after a burn, are their usual habitats. These plants are phenomenal honey producing plants when dense enough, making honey which is sought after by honey lovers and health enthusiasts who value its medicinal properties. The plants are temperature and weather hardy, and can be found from the Arctic and subarctic areas in the north, down through Europe, and into the northern regions of Africa.

Heather honey is known for the nectar’s powerful and characteristic aroma and flavor. For this reason, it is prized honey, in great demand and generally expensive. For those who adore Heather Honey however, there is no substitute. Heather honey can vary from region to region and from heather species to heather species, which means Heather Honey can take on characteristics of the specific heather types, but there are some characteristics they have in common.

Heather Honey has a powerful and very distinctive scent, often described as herbal, woody, warm, floral, earthy, and even as a “flowery resinous aroma,” sometimes with very faint, but still characteristic caramel notes. The smell of Heather Honey is often compared to the perfume scent given off by the heather flowers. The taste is often described as having smoky and peppery notes, being full-bodied, highly aromatic, spicy, pungent, tangy and warm, with a very lingering aftertaste and with a distinctively sharp, very bitter and even a very “pungent” taste, that can catch one by surprise if you aren’t used to it. For this reason, Heather Honey is usually an acquired taste and is often described as having slight medicinal, woody and even “animal” notes. Heather Honeys have pronounced flavor tones and are often noted as being the most strongly flavored honeys. Both Calluna (ling) and Erica Heather Honeys are rich in pollen and essential vitamins, stimulating physical strength and rejuvenating the body. Heath and Heather are used as a stimulant in herbal stimulants such as “erica tea,” which is said to restore stamina and strength. They are also used in medicinal teas such as “herbal heather tea,” which is used to boost the immune system and to detoxify the body. Heather honey is also believed to help increase the intake of nutrients and minerals into the system, because of the way it acts with the body. Heather Honey has a long history as a medicinal honey, and it has been considered one of the most beneficial honeys for centuries, due to its high level of antioxidants and other phytochemicals. Heather Honey is often used as a treatment for respiratory illnesses and throat infections. It is also used as an effective anti-inflammatory agent, and is used to boost the immune system and support heart function. Heather Honey is believed to be helpful in treating anemia, rheumatism, arthritis, sleep difficulties and insomnia, digestive difficulties and liver function. It has also long been believed to be helpful in lowering blood pressure and is used as a tonic to restore circulation. Heather Honey has a mild laxative effect that has been used to relieve constipation. These medical benefits, historically known and used by locals, have long been used to justify the high cost of Heather Honey. Historically speaking, the demand for Heather Honey was much greater than the supply. Even today this is true in some areas and in some years; in other areas, there is an abundance of Heather Honey, and those who are fortunate enough to live in such places can often obtain it at lower costs.

Heather Honey tends to have a dark amber color, sometimes with orange or red tints, and is often compared to the color of dark malt beers. Its consistency is one of the unique features of Heather Honey. It is normally described as a jelly-like consistency, thixotropic, thick and viscous, only becoming fluid when stirred or agitated. Because of this gelatinous quality, Heather Honey has a higher water content than most honeys and yet resists fermentation. It also resists crystallization, remaining soft and spreadable. All of these characteristics make Heather Honey somewhat more difficult to extract from the comb and bottle than some other types of honey, and special methods are used by beekeepers to deal with this.

In the case of Calluna Heather, in particular, this thixotropic quality is one of the ways to know if one has obtained true Ling Heather Honey. When this honey is in the comb, a knife sliced into the comb will reveal that it has a gelatinous consistency; it does not drip very much, and often can be easier to obtain as a comb honey than as an extracted honey. Beekeepers must often press the honey out of the combs or use special equipment to stir the honey, or they may leave it as comb honey or cut-comb honey, in order to preserve its unique qualities.

Heather Honey is prized in cooking and as a table honey in northern European countries, especially in Scotland, England, Ireland, Norway and parts of France, Germany, Denmark and other countries where Calluna and Erica heaths and heathers grow. Many people enjoy Heather Honey on bread or toast, with cheese, in yogurt, in warm porridge, or drizzled over fruit. It is especially lovely with strong cheeses such as blue cheeses and aged cheddars, and with smoked meats, roasted meats and game. It is sometimes paired with whisky or used in glazes and marinades for meats. Heather Honey is also used in various desserts, cakes, biscuits and puddings.

Historically, Heather Honey has also been used as a prized ingredient in “heather mead” and other fermented drinks, and has a long tradition in brewing and in making heather ales and meads. The Picts, an ancient people of Scotland, are said to have used heather and Heather Honey in brewing a highly regarded heather ale, a tradition that continued into later times and which has recently seen a revival among some craft brewers.

Calluna Heather, also known as Ling Heather (Calluna vulgaris), is the iconic heather plant that many people think of when they hear the word “heather.” It is a low-growing shrub that blankets hillsides and moors in shades of pink and purple when in bloom. Ling Heather is the dominant heather in many heathlands and moorlands and is a major nectar source for bees. In some areas, entire landscapes turn purple in late summer when the Ling Heather blooms, and the bees produce monofloral Ling Heather Honey. Ling Heather is particularly renowned for producing thixotropic honey with a very distinctive flavor and aroma. It is also of great ecological importance, providing habitat and forage for many species of insects, birds and other wildlife.

The Erica heathers, often called Bell Heathers, include Erica cinerea (Bell Heather), Erica tetralix (Cross-leaved Heath), Erica arborea (Tree Heath or White Heather), and others. These also produce significant nectar flows and contribute to Heather Honey. In some regions, the Bell Heathers bloom slightly earlier than Ling Heather and can produce their own Bell Heather honeys or mixed Heather Honeys. Erica Honeys can be slightly different in flavor and aroma from Ling Heather honey, sometimes being a bit lighter or less intense, but there are still strongly flavored and appreciated by those who enjoy Heather Honey. Some Erica species, such as Erica arborea, can also produce lighter-colored honeys with delicate flavor nuances.

Heather Honey production depends on the right weather conditions, the density of the heather stands, and the movement and management of hives. Beekeepers often move their hives into heather moors and heathlands in late summer, following the bloom, in order to produce Heather Honey. In some countries, such as Scotland, England and Norway, there are traditions of transhumance beekeeping where hives are moved from lowland clover or wildflower pastures up into the heather moors as the season progresses. The success of a Heather Honey crop can vary greatly from year to year depending on weather, with wet, cold or stormy conditions during the heather bloom reducing nectar production and bee foraging.

Heather Honey is closely tied to the cultural identity of some regions, especially in Scotland, where heather-covered hills and moors are iconic landscapes. Heather itself is often regarded as a symbol of Scotland and is one of the national flowers of Norway.
